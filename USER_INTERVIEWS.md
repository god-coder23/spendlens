## Interview 1

**Name / initials:** Withheld on request  
**Role:** Founder  
**Company stage:** Early-stage SaaS startup (6-person team)  

**Direct quotes:**
- "We started with one AI subscription per developer, and now we’re paying for four different tools without really questioning it."
- "Nobody on the team actually owns AI spend. The charges just get approved every month."
- "I care less about finding the cheapest tool and more about avoiding unnecessary overlap."
- "If your recommendations sound too aggressive, I immediately stop trusting the product."

**Most surprising thing they said:**  
The founder explained that even when they suspect they are overspending, they avoid removing tools because developers push back against losing workflows they already rely on. Operational comfort mattered more than saving small amounts of money.

**What it changed about my design:**  
Originally, the audit engine strongly pushed tool replacements. After this conversation, I shifted the recommendations toward safer optimizations like downgrades, seat reductions, and identifying duplicate subscriptions before suggesting major workflow changes. I also softened the language on the results page so it sounds more like financial guidance than AI marketing copy.

## Interview 2

**Name / initials:** Anonymous  
**Role:** Solo founder  
**Company stage:** Pre-revenue indie project  

**Direct quotes:**
- "The dangerous spending isn’t subscriptions. It’s random API usage that slowly stacks up."
- "I don’t really know my exact monthly AI spend unless I manually check invoices."
- "Honestly, if every result says I can save money, I’d assume the tool is fake."
- "I’d trust a calculator more than an AI chatbot making recommendations."

**Most surprising thing they said:**  
The founder said they would trust the product more if it occasionally told users their setup was already reasonable instead of always forcing a savings opportunity. They viewed honesty as more valuable than maximizing lead generation.

**What it changed about my design:**  
I added support for “healthy spending” outcomes where the audit can honestly say the current setup is already efficient. I also made the savings explanations more numbers-driven and less opinionated so the recommendations feel defensible instead of subjective.

## Interview 3

**Name / initials:** Private  
**Role:** Engineering manager  
**Company stage:** Seed-stage AI startup (12-person team)  

**Direct quotes:**
- "The issue is not buying AI tools. The issue is nobody cleans them up later."
- "API spending becomes invisible because it’s spread across multiple projects."
- "I don’t want another analytics dashboard. I want one clear recommendation."
- "Benchmarks would actually be useful. I want to know if our spend per developer is normal or not."

**Most surprising thing they said:**  
The engineering manager mentioned that AI spending discussions usually happen only after finance teams notice unusual invoices, not during regular engineering planning. Cost optimization was reactive instead of proactive.

**What it changed about my design:**  
This conversation pushed me to simplify the audit results into a single high-level recommendation before showing detailed breakdowns. I also added the idea of future benchmarking features comparing spend per developer across similar startup sizes and workflows.
