# Economics

If Credex deployed SpendLens tomorrow, the economics would work only if the tool behaves like a qualified lead generator rather than a broad top-of-funnel content toy. So I would start with one simplifying assumption: the value of a converted lead is driven by the margin Credex earns on an AI credit purchase after the audit identifies overspend and the user books a consultation.

Here is a rough but workable model. Assume the typical “high-savings” user is a 10-person software team spending around $1,500 per month on AI tools and API usage. If SpendLens identifies 20% savings, that is about $300 per month of visible pain. Now assume Credex closes those users into a credit purchase or discounted procurement relationship worth $6,000 in annual billed volume. If Credex captures a 20% gross margin on that relationship, the annual gross profit on the converted account is about `$1,200`. That makes one closed customer worth roughly `$1,200` in contribution margin before servicing costs.

Now look at CAC by channel from the GTM plan. For founder DMs and manual outreach, media CAC is basically `$0`, but labor CAC is not. If it takes 8 hours of founder/operator time to source, post, DM, and follow up with enough prospects to generate 10 completed audits, and I value that time at `$40/hour`, that is `$320` of labor spend, or `$32` per completed audit. If 15% of completed audits book a consultation and 20% of those consultations close, the blended close rate from completed audit to purchase is `3%`. At `$32` per completed audit, the acquisition cost per customer becomes roughly `$1,067` (`$32 / 0.03`). That is barely profitable against a `$1,200` gross-profit account, which means manual outreach only works if the close rate is better or the deal size is larger.

That is why the best version of this tool needs strong conversion on high-savings users. A more durable model is:

- `audit completed -> consultation booked`: 12%
- `consultation booked -> credit purchase`: 30%
- total `audit completed -> purchase`: 3.6%

At that conversion rate, to make a `$32` audit acquisition cost work well, Credex needs at least about `$900` to `$1,000` of gross profit per closed customer. That is plausible if the target account is not a tiny solo user but an actual startup team with recurring AI spend.

To drive `$1M ARR` in 18 months, I would model it backward. Suppose the average closed customer contributes `$8,000 ARR` to Credex. Then Credex needs about `125 active customers`. If the audit-to-purchase conversion rate is `3.6%`, then Credex needs about `3,472 completed audits` over that period. That is about `193 audits per month` on average, which is achievable if the tool becomes a repeatable distribution wedge rather than a one-time launch gimmick.

What would have to be true for that to work? Three things. First, the tool must consistently identify meaningful savings for a subset of users, especially teams already paying enough for Credex to matter. Second, the lead capture must happen after the user sees value, so completion-to-email conversion stays healthy. Third, Credex must route high-savings users into a fast human follow-up motion. If those three things hold, SpendLens can be economically credible. If not, it becomes a nice demo that does not compound.
