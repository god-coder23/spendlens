# GTM

The exact target user for SpendLens is a founder-led or engineering-led startup with 5 to 40 employees, already paying for multiple AI tools, and just beginning to feel subscription sprawl. The best initial persona is a technical founder, engineering manager, or head of product at a seed to Series A software company that ships weekly, experiments fast, and lets teams buy tools bottom-up. This user is not casually browsing “AI.” They are already paying for ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor, Copilot, or API usage, and they suspect they are overspending but have not made time to audit it.

Right before they want this tool, they are usually doing one of a few very specific things: reviewing SaaS spend in a finance spreadsheet, deciding whether to expand a team-wide AI license, comparing Cursor vs Copilot vs Claude workflows in Slack, or searching things like “Cursor vs Copilot for team,” “best AI coding tool pricing,” “ChatGPT Team worth it,” or “AI tool stack for startup engineering team.” They are also highly likely to click a tweet or Hacker News post with a strong quantified hook like “We found $640/month in wasted AI spend across a 9-person dev team.”

The places they hang out are not generic “social media.” I would start with:

- Hacker News comment threads on AI dev tools and engineering budgets
- r/startups, r/SaaS, r/Entrepreneur, and r/LocalLLaMA when the thread is about tooling cost or workflow
- Indie Hackers posts and comments, especially founder update threads
- Small founder and engineering Slacks where AI tool discussions happen in public channels
- X lists that track AI engineers, technical founders, and devtool builders
- Communities around Cursor, Claude Code, Windsurf, and GitHub Copilot where stack comparisons happen naturally

To get the first 100 users in 30 days with $0 paid budget, I would not start with “content marketing.” I would run four concrete plays. First, I would publish 10 teardown posts showing anonymized example audits for different startup profiles: 3-person dev agency, 8-person SaaS team, 15-person support-heavy company, and so on. Second, I would DM people who publicly complain about AI tool bills and offer a free audit link with one sentence of context. Third, I would post a “we audited our own AI stack and found X” style thread with screenshots that drive directly into the tool. Fourth, I would ask friendly founders and student builders to run their own stacks through the tool and repost their results if they found savings.

The unfair distribution channel is Credex itself. Credex already sits inside the exact workflow where teams realize they are overpaying and want discounted credits or alternate purchasing paths. That means the audit tool does not need to invent a monetization story from scratch. It can be distributed through operator communities, AI infra conversations, and credit-related content in a way that generic budgeting tools cannot replicate. A standalone founder would have to fight for trust. Credex can say, credibly, “we already help companies reduce these bills.”

If week 1 traction is real, it should look like numbers, not vibes. For me that means:

- 200 unique landing-page visitors
- 60 completed audits
- 15 shared result links
- 10 email captures
- 4 consultation-intent leads with projected savings above $500/month

That would be enough to prove that the tool is doing more than entertaining people. It would show that a real percentage of visitors are willing to input their current stack, trust the output, and take the next step.
